What is an incident postmortem?

Incident postmortems are really important and are used by teams that build software/hardware to understand how to combat failure and make systems more resilient.

An incident postmortem is an exercise in learning how to make things like hardware, software, processes better after an incident.

An incident is really anything that has happened in your software that has affected anyone who uses it adversely.

Incident postmortems are really important and are used by teams that build software/hardware to understand how to combat failure and make systems more resilient. They’re also really important for customers or consumers of products and services so they have insight into what has been happening.

 

Customers lose trust in companies if they are kept in the dark when the products they pay for fail.

 

The end to this post by Daniel Doubrovkine former CTO at Artsy really hammers home the impact honest communication can have for customers and consumers: https://artsy.github.io/blog/2014/11/19/how-to-write-great-outage-post-mortems/

By scrutinising what happens to a system that causes it to fail, a team can understand how not to have that happen again. That makes teams happier, and customers who use their software or services happier because it’s more likely to be reliable.

Here’s a really simple incident postmortem from Google: https://status.cloud.google.com/incident/storage/19002

Incident postmortems have a few key things they need to include. In our Google example above, they have:

 

  • Summary
  • Detailed description of the impact
  • Root Cause
  • Remediation and prevention

 

One of the last sections of that is probably the most important piece of an incident postmortem. Remediation and Prevention is, “How we fixed it”, and “How we’ll not have this happen ever again!”
